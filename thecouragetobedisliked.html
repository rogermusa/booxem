Adlerian Psychology: Freedom from the Past

"The Courage to Be Disliked" presents the psychological and philosophical ideas of Alfred Adler, a contemporary of Freud and Jung, through an engaging dialogue between a philosopher and a youth. It fundamentally challenges conventional thinking about happiness, freedom, and interpersonal relationships.

A core tenet is the rejection of etiology (the study of causation), particularly Freudian trauma theory. Adlerian psychology argues that past experiences, while influential, do not determine our present. We are not defined by trauma, but by the meaning we assign to past events. Instead, it embraces teleology (the study of purpose) – the idea that our behavior is driven by the goals we set for ourselves, consciously or unconsciously.

Unhappiness, anxiety, and other negative states are often unconsciously chosen by the individual to achieve certain goals (like avoiding responsibility, seeking attention, or justifying inaction). The power to change lies entirely in the present moment.

"No experience is in itself a cause of our success or failure. We do not suffer from the shock of our experiences—the so-called trauma—but instead we make out of them whatever suits our purposes."

— Alfred Adler (via the book)

Key Principles for Living Freely

Several interconnected principles form the foundation of Adlerian psychology as presented in the book:

Separation of Tasks

All interpersonal problems arise from confusing one's own tasks with those of others. Your task is what you directly control (your effort, choices, attitude). Others' tasks are their reactions, feelings, and judgments. Do not intervene in others' tasks, and do not allow others to intervene in yours. This creates true freedom from seeking approval.

Community Feeling (Kōkenkan)

True happiness is found in Community Feeling – the sense of belonging and contribution to a larger whole (family, society, humanity). This involves seeing others as comrades, not competitors, and feeling "I am useful to someone." It requires self-acceptance and trust in others.

Self-Acceptance

Focus on what you can change and accept what you cannot. Self-acceptance means acknowledging your "unable-to-do" aspects without judgment, and focusing your energy on developing what you can. It's about accepting your irreplaceable "60 percent" self and moving forward, distinct from positive thinking that ignores reality.

Confidence in Others (Trust)

Unconditionally trusting others, seeing them as comrades rather than enemies, is essential for community feeling. This doesn't mean being naive about potential betrayal, but choosing to trust as your task, regardless of how they behave (their task). This builds deeper relationships.

Contribution to Others

Feeling useful and making tangible contributions to the community (your comrades) is the practical path to realizing community feeling and achieving happiness. This feeling of contribution gives you a sense of worth.

Focus on the Here and Now

Life is a series of moments. Focus intensely on the here and now, like dancing, without being bound by past interpretations or future anxieties. Life is not a line towards a destination, but a continuous series of present moments to be lived fully.

Implications for Freedom and Happiness

Applying these principles leads to profound shifts in perspective:

  • Freedom from Interpersonal Problems: By separating tasks, you stop seeking external recognition and stop worrying about others' opinions. Your worth is self-determined.
  • Rejection of Recognition Seeking: Living to satisfy others' expectations is enslavement. True freedom means living according to your own principles, even if it invites dislike.
  • Horizontal Relationships: Viewing relationships as equal partnerships (horizontal) rather than hierarchical (vertical), avoiding praise and rebuke, fostering mutual respect and encouragement.
  • Focus on the Process, Not the Goal: Seeing life as a journey of moments rather than a race to a destination. Enjoying the process ("dancing") in the here and now leads to fulfillment.

The Courage to Choose Freedom

"The Courage to Be Disliked" offers a liberating and actionable philosophy for achieving happiness and freedom. It argues that our lives are not predetermined by past events or external circumstances, but are shaped by the goals we choose and the meaning we create.

By embracing the separation of tasks, cultivating community feeling, accepting ourselves, and focusing on contribution in the present moment, we can break free from the chains of interpersonal conflict and the quest for external validation. This requires courage – specifically, the courage to live authentically and be disliked by those who disapprove of our choices.

Happiness is not something to be attained in the future; it is a state of being, accessible right now through contribution, connection, and the courage to live freely.
